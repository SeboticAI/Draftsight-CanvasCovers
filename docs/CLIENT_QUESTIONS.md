## 1. Wall geometry — THE BLOCKER

Sit with the measurer over the page-2 LEFT WALL sheet, point at the
labels, have them narrate what each one represents on the actual
finished lift blanket.

The labels:

```
LLR = 300
LR3 = 400,  LR2 = 1400,  LR1 = 600        (top, TOTAL LENGTH = 2400)
L1  = 1450, L2  = 240,   L3  = 350        (side, TOTAL LENGTH = 2040)
LRR = 300
```

**Ask:**

- Walk us through one wall — what is each label physically? Our best
  guess: `LLR` / `LRR` are short door-return tabs at the outer
  corners that fold around the door pillars; `LR1`/`LR2`/`LR3` are
  fold-segments along the top edge; `L1`/`L2`/`L3` are fold-segments
  along the side edge. Right?
- Why are the top sum (2400) and side sum (2040) different sizes? Is
  the cut piece a rectangle 2400 × 2040, or a stepped / trapezoidal
  shape we're not seeing?
- How many segments can a wall actually have? Always 1 + 3 + 3 + 1 =
  eight, or does it vary by job?
- What's the functional difference between an `LR` segment and an
  `L` segment? Stitch lines vs folds? Different fold directions?
- Does the cutter emit one perimeter polyline with internal fold
  lines, or separate polylines per segment?

**Why this is the blocker:** our current 3-DR-on-one-side model
doesn't fit any of this. A real lift blanket cut from our tool today
wouldn't actually fit the lift. Without these answers, no more
geometry code lands.

## 2. The math — the wow-factor opportunity

The form handwriting confirms the operator is doing this every job.

**Ask:**

- Should the tool take measured height and emit cut height
  automatically? Or do you want to keep doing the math by hand?
- Is the fixing-allowance list complete: `HOOKS = -50`,
  `PRESS STUDS = -40`, `EYELET TG9 = -30`, `EYELET TG7 = -30`,
  `VELCRO = 0`? Anything else (BUNGEE? KEDER?)?
- What does `EYELET TG9` vs `EYELET TG7` mean — different gauges?
  Per-job or per-product choice?
- Is `+10mm` width universal, or does it depend on fabric weight,
  fixing type, product?
- What does `×2` actually mean physically — fold-over, double layer?
  Universal across products or lift-blanket-specific?

**Why it matters:** automating this is the single biggest time-saving
move and the easiest "wow" in a demo follow-up. If the answer to #1
is "yes, automate it" — that's Phase 1.

## 3. Vertical and horizontal quilting

The lookup table on the side of their DXF (`4700 = 783`, `4500 = 750`,
`4300 = 716`, `4100 = 683`) is roughly `width / 6` but doesn't fit
cleanly.

**Ask:**

- Is it a printed lookup chart they read from, or is there a formula
  behind it?
- Should quilt lines appear on every drawing, or only when the
  operator ticks a box? On which layer?
- Does horizontal quilting exist too? The legend mentions `H QUILT`.
- Quilt the main panel only, or door returns too?

## 4. Other product types and the unused layers

The DXFs define `2 Drag Blade`, `3 Crease Tool`, `4 Drill Tool` but
never use them in lift-blanket jobs.

**Ask:**

- Which product types use them? Caravan annexes? Storm flaps?
- Are the layer names with leading digits (`1 Rotary Blade`) strict,
  or does the cutter read by sort order?
- What's the layer convention for: caravan annexes, bag awnings,
  mesh walls, bed-end bag flys, storm flaps, caravan covers, ute
  canopies? (These came up in the original brief as future
  candidates.)
